A Multi-Center Study on eFAST Learning Curves for Emergency Medicine Residents Using CUSUM Analysis
Roppolo LP et al. · AEM Education and Training · May 2026 · PMID 42110192
This multicenter retrospective study used CUSUM analysis to track eFAST competency in 29 EM interns across three programs, finding median initial competency after just 8 scans (range 5–15)—far fewer than the current 25-scan ACGME benchmark. Prior POCUS experience did not significantly affect the number of scans needed. The findings directly challenge existing training requirements and support moving toward individualized, competency-based eFAST assessment rather than fixed scan counts.
Emergency medicine (EM) interns have been required to complete a minimum of 25 eFAST (Extended Focused Assessment with Sonography in Trauma) scans during residency training, despite limited evidence supporting this numeric benchmark. We hypothesized that learners will achieve initial eFAST competency in less than 25 scans. We performed a multicenter retrospective review of prospectively gathered quality assurance (QA) data from eFAST scans performed by EM interns during their one-month ultrasound rotation at three sites using the same eFAST curriculum and QA rubric. The median number of scans to achieve competency per LC-CUSUM analysis was 8 (IQR [5, 10]) with a range of 5–15. Using the Wilcoxon rank sum analysis, there was no significant difference in scans required for competency between interns with and without prior eFAST experience (p = 0.85). LC-CUSUM analysis demonstrated that our EM interns achieved initial eFAST competency after a median of 8 scans (range 5–15).

The predictive value of different lung ultrasound protocols for extravascular lung water in septic shock patients
Ding Y et al. · The Ultrasound Journal · 2026-05-29 · PMID 42212423
In 46 septic shock patients undergoing PiCCO monitoring, this study compared three LUS protocols—4-region, 8-region, and BLUE—for predicting extravascular lung water index (EVLWI). The BLUE protocol showed the strongest correlation with EVLWI (r = 0.634) and performed best on ROC analysis for detecting, diagnosing, and grading pulmonary edema severity. This supports use of the BLUE protocol as the preferred LUS approach for lung water monitoring in critically ill septic patients.
BACKGROUND: Currently, the role of lung ultrasound (LUS) in the diagnosis and treatment of patients with septic shock is widely recognized. Various LUS protocols and scoring criteria have been proposed, yet a unified LUS protocol for assessing lung water in these patients remains lacking. METHODS: Forty-six septic shock patients underwent LUS with three scanning schemes (4-region, 8-region, BLUE) alongside pulse indicated continuous cardiac output (PiCCO) monitoring. Spearman correlation analysis was used to compare the correlation between the three ultrasound scoring schemes, PiCCO and other clinical laboratory indicators. RESULTS: The BLUE protocol showed a stronger correlation with EVLWI than the 4-region protocol and the 8-region protocol (r=0.634, 0.458, 0.546, p<0.001). The ROC curve analysis showed that ultrasound protocols could predict the early occurrence of pulmonary edema in septic shock patients (EVLWI>7 ml/kg), diagnosis of pulmonary edema (EVLWI>10 ml/kg), and evaluation of pulmonary fluid severity (EVLWI≥15 ml/kg). CONCLUSIONS: Among the 4-region, 8-region and BLUE protocols, the BLUE protocol shows relatively better overall performance in predicting lung water elevation and evaluating edema severity.

A Predictive Model for Differential Diagnosis of Cardiogenic and Non-cardiogenic Dyspnea in CKD Based on Cardio and Lung Ultrasound Features
Zhao H et al. · J Ultrasound Med · 2026-05-27 · PMID 42200685
Using 215 training and 56 validation CKD patients, this study built a combined cardiac + lung ultrasound nomogram to differentiate cardiogenic pulmonary edema from non-cardiogenic dyspnea, achieving AUC 0.97 (training) and 0.89 (validation). Key discriminators included E/e', IVC diameter, bilateral B-line pattern, and consolidation. Provides a practical, validated bedside tool for avoiding misdiagnosis and optimizing fluid management decisions in this challenging patient population.
BACKGROUND: Lung ultrasound (LUS) quantifying B-lines is valuable to assess fluid status during hemodialysis. However, B-lines may indicate not only cardiogenic pulmonary edema (CPE) but also non-cardiogenic pulmonary diseases, posing challenges to guiding fluid therapy decisions. METHODS: This retrospective observational study enrolled chronic kidney disease (CKD) stage 3-5 patients. Multivariable logistic regression identified discriminative echocardiographic and LUS parameters. RESULTS: Multivariable regression identified E/e' (OR 1.180), inferior vena cava diameter (OR 1.240), bilateral symmetric B-pattern (OR 9.720), bilateral continuous B-pattern (OR 13.777), ≥1 irregular pleural line region (OR 0.078), ≥1 consolidation region (OR 0.039), ≥1 pleural effusion region (OR 4.732) as independent CPE predictors. The nomogram showed AUC of 0.9703 (training) and 0.8910 (validation). CONCLUSIONS: The nomogram model demonstrated that ultrasound parameters and β-weighted scoring system possess reliable clinical application value and high predictive accuracy.

Sosa Barrios RH et al. · Blood Purification · 2026-05-28 · PMID 42207730
This pilot study applied a combined VExUS + lung ultrasound protocol in 31 kidney transplant patients in the first week post-transplant, identifying subclinical congestion in 28.6% of patients with a normal physical exam and driving treatment modification in 74% on Day +1. Physical exam showed poor agreement with VExUS (κ=0.043) but moderate agreement with LUS (κ=0.469). Hypothesis-generating evidence that multimodal POCUS outperforms clinical exam alone for volume status in post-transplant management.
Background: Accurate volume assessment during immediate post-kidney transplantation period reduces morbidity and mortality. Lung ultrasound (LUS), VExUS score, and bioimpedance (BIA) are emerging methods for volume evaluation. Method: Exploratory, prospective, single-centre pilot study (Group 1, n = 31) using VExUS + LUS protocol on days +1 and +7 post-transplantation, compared with historical reference cohort (Group 2, n = 40). BMC Med Educ EM Practice ○ Preliminary
Iterative development of a pediatric point-of-care ultrasound training program
Kerenyi A et al. · BMC Med Educ · 2026-05-29 · PMID 42215982
Over five years and nine course iterations, 136 physicians participated in a pediatric POCUS curriculum developed using Kern's 6-step model. The curriculum stabilized after three iterations, ultimately centering on supervised real-patient scanning. Integration of the ImageSim online platform enabled asynchronous image interpretation skill development. Learners who saved more scans initially were most likely to achieve certification. Practical blueprint for any institution building a pediatric POCUS program from scratch.
BACKGROUND: Point-of-care ultrasound (POCUS) is increasingly used across pediatric subspecialties. However, training opportunities in pediatric POCUS remain limited, and few studies have detailed the curriculum development process. METHODS: We employed Kern's 6-step approach to curriculum development to design a pediatric POCUS training program. A mixed-methods analysis of participant feedback was used for the iterative development. RESULTS: Between 2018 and 2023, 136 physicians participated in nine course iterations, providing 347 survey responses. The curriculum reached its final format after three iterations. Most in-person contact time was dedicated to supervised hands-on scanning. The inclusion of the online learning platform ImageSim enabled learners to develop image interpretation skills asynchronously. Learners participating in the final curriculum format and those who saved most scans initially were most likely to get certified. CONCLUSIONS: Even in the absence of preexisting POCUS experience, our iterative curriculum development process led to the creation of a successful pediatric POCUS course.

Point-of-care ultrasound for emergency pericardiocentesis: a multidimensional CUSUM analysis of the learning curve
Tang X et al. · BMC Cardiovasc Disord · 2026-05-26 · PMID 42192501
In 33 consecutive US-guided pericardiocentesis cases by a single novice operator, CUSUM analysis identified a skill-acquisition to skill-proficiency inflection at approximately 14 cases. Complication rates were higher in phase 1 (28.6% vs 5.3%), and procedure time and operator confidence improved significantly after ~14 cases. Emergency point-of-care ultrasound-guided percutaneous pericardial catheter drainage (US-GPPCD) is a life-saving intervention for cardiac tamponade, yet the learning curve among novice operators in emergency settings remains poorly characterized. A total of 33 consecutive patients undergoing US-GPPCD between October 2024 and December 2025 by a single novice operator without prior independent experience were enrolled. CUSUM analysis divided the learning curve into two phases: phase 1 (cases 1–14, skill-acquisition) and phase 2 (cases 15–33, skill-proficiency). Sonographic pleural line excursion in children with bronchospasm: a prospective single-center study
Scheier E et al. · Respir Med · 2026-05-26 · PMID 42203185
In 50 children presenting to a pediatric ED with acute wheeze, M-mode POCUS measured pleural line excursion as a quantitative asthma severity marker. A combined model of excursion + SpO₂ + heart rate predicted hospital admission with AUC 0.81. Among hypoxic children, excursion ≤2.0 mm predicted 73% admission rate vs 17% for excursion >2.0 mm. Novel objective POCUS metric for quantifying bronchospasm severity—requires prospective validation.
Pediatric asthma scoring relies on subjective findings with poor interrater reliability. We prospectively studied children presenting to a pediatric emergency department with acute wheeze requiring beta-agonist therapy, measuring pleural line excursion (maximum minus minimum vertical displacement of the pleural line) from M-mode ultrasound video using a custom algorithm. Fifty children were analyzed. A multivariate logistic regression model constructed to predict hospital admission, combining excursion with peripheral capillary oxygen saturation (SpO₂), age-adjusted heart-rate percentile and age, achieved an area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) of 0.81. Among hypoxic children (SpO₂ ≤91.5%), excursion ≤2.0 mm conferred a 73% admission rate versus 17% for excursion >2.0 mm. These exploratory findings require prospective validation.
J Am Assoc Nurse Pract EM Practice ○ Preliminary
Eye tracking to understand performance in point-of-care lung ultrasound: Insights from nurse practitioners training
Lavoie P et al. · J Am Assoc Nurse Pract · 2026-05-26 · PMID 42201345
Mixed-methods study using eye-tracking glasses in 35 nurse practitioners learning lung POCUS via simulation, with three expert comparators. Experts had faster image acquisition and more focused gaze patterns. Lateral and basal lung zones were most challenging; higher adiposity impaired scanning. Eye tracking associated with post-training confidence gains and OSAUS scores correlated with interpretation accuracy. Introduces eye tracking as a novel objective assessment modality for POCUS education research.
Teaching and assessing lung point-of-care ultrasound (POCUS) requires attention to both image acquisition and interpretation. Eye tracking offers an objective approach to examining visual attention during scanning. This convergent mixed-methods study used eye tracking to characterize visual attention and performance during lung POCUS training. Thirty-five nurse practitioners completed simulation-based lung POCUS training while wearing eye-tracking glasses; three experts completed the same protocol for comparison. Experts demonstrated faster image acquisition and more focused gaze patterns than learners. Performance varied across lung zones, with lateral and basal regions presenting the greatest challenges. Higher gaze-based performance was associated with greater post-training confidence, and OSAUS scores were associated with interpretation accuracy. When used as an analytical approach, eye tracking provides complementary insight into scanning performance and may help inform targeted, site-specific lung POCUS training strategies.

Automated Echocardiographic Detection of Congenital Heart Disease Using Artificial Intelligence
Lukyanenko P et al. · Circulation · 2026-03-28 · PMID 41902792
EchoFocus-CHD, an AI model trained on 3.4 million echo videos from 54,727 studies at Boston Children's Hospital, detected critical congenital heart disease with AUROC 0.94 internally and improved to 0.87 on international cohorts after retraining. The model correctly prioritized clinically relevant echo views (PLAX, PSAX, subxiphoid, apical) and showed robust lesion-level detection across 20 CHD subtypes. A landmark study demonstrating AI's potential to democratize expert pediatric echo interpretation globally.
BACKGROUND: Delayed or missed diagnosis of congenital heart disease (CHD) contributes to excess pediatric mortality worldwide. Echocardiography (echo) is central to diagnosing and triaging CHD, yet expert interpretation remains a scarce and maldistributed global resource. METHODS: We developed EchoFocus-CHD, an AI-enabled model for automated detection of 12 critical and 8 noncritical CHD lesions. The model was internally trained and tested on the first echo per patient from Boston Children's Hospital, with further evaluation on a referral cohort of echo studies performed at external US and international centers. RESULTS: The internal and referral cohorts included 3.4 million videos from 54,727 echos and 167,484 videos from 3,356 echos respectively. EchoFocus-CHD showed excellent internal ability to detect composite critical CHD (AUROC, 0.94; PLR, 7.50; NLR, 0.14). Performance declined on the referral cohort (AUROC, 0.77), coinciding with greater expert disagreement. Retraining improved international critical CHD detection (AUROC, 0.87). CONCLUSIONS: EchoFocus-CHD shows promise for automated CHD detection to advance equitable global cardiovascular care.

BACKGROUND: Heart failure is prevalent; however, there is no cost-effective screening option. Ultrasound-guided percutaneous needle fenestration (UPNF) and percutaneous needle tenotomy (UPNT) are minimally invasive procedures commonly used to treat chronic tendinopathies. However, these techniques can be painful, potentially limiting patient tolerance, satisfaction, and procedural feasibility. The objectives of this study are to propose a practical, anatomy-based approach for the use of ultrasound-guided peripheral nerve blocks (USG-PNBs) as a strategy to reduce pain and improve patient comfort during UPNF and UPNT. A set of targeted USG-PNBs was defined for commonly treated tendinopathies, including supraspinatus tendinopathy, lateral and medial epicondylitis, gluteus medius and/or minimus tendinopathy, patellar tendinopathy, Achilles tendinopathy, and plantar fasciopathy. Ultrasound-guided peripheral nerve blocks represent a practical and effective approach to manage pain during percutaneous procedures for chronic tendinopathies.
