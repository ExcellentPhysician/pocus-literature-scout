Point-of-Care Ultrasound for Pediatric Urethral Catheterization: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Randomized Controlled Trials
Vieira da Silva HF et al. 📅 2026-06 PMID: 42246462
This meta-analysis of 3 RCTs (337 participants) found that real-time POCUS-guided urethral catheterization in children ≤36 months significantly improved first-attempt success (89.7% vs. 72.5%, RR 1.25, NNT=6) and reduced futile "dry tap" attempts (3.6% vs. 23.9%, NNT=5 to prevent one futile attempt). Caregiver satisfaction was higher and overall workflow times were comparable. This provides actionable RCT-level evidence for integrating pre-catheterization bladder scanning into routine pediatric emergency practice.
Abstract (verbatim from PubMed) Urethral catheterization is the standard method for obtaining sterile urine in nontoilet-trained children, but the conventional blind technique frequently results in futile attempts ("dry taps") on empty bladders. Point-of-care ultrasound (POCUS) offers the clinical advantage of confirming adequate urine volume to prevent mucosal trauma and procedural distress. However, its routine implementation may be limited by operator training requirements and potential workflow delays while waiting for bladder filling. Therefore, this study aims to evaluate the efficacy of real-time POCUS-assisted versus standard blind urethral catheterization in pediatric patients. We conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials (RCTs) adhering to PRISMA guidelines (PROSPERO: CRD420251247483). We searched PubMed, Embase, and CENTRAL for trials comparing POCUS-guided versus standard catheterization in children aged ≤ 36 months. The primary outcome was the first-attempt success rate. The certainty of evidence was evaluated using the GRADE approach. Three trials comprising 337 participants were included. POCUS-assisted catheterization significantly increased first-attempt success compared to the conventional technique (89.7% vs. 72.5%; RR 1.25, 95% CI 1.08-1.45, p = 0.0022), yielding a Number Needed to Treat (NNT) of 6. Furthermore, ultrasound guidance significantly reduced the rate of "dry taps" (3.6% vs. 23.9%; RR 0.25, 95% CI 0.13-0.47, p < 0.0001), representing an NNT of 5 to prevent one futile attempt. The certainty of evidence for the primary outcome was rated as moderate. Secondary descriptive outcomes indicated higher caregiver satisfaction, lower perceived patient discomfort, and comparable overall workflow times despite required waiting periods for bladder filling. Real-time POCUS significantly improves first-attempt catheterization success and drastically reduces futile attempts in young children. Furthermore, it enhances caregiver satisfaction and minimizes patient distress without intrinsically delaying emergency department workflow. These highly actionable findings support the integration of ultrasound guidance into routine pediatric emergency care.

A Predictive Model for Differential Diagnosis of Cardiogenic and Non-cardiogenic Dyspnea in Patients with Chronic Kidney Disease Based on Cardio and Lung Ultrasound Features
Zhao H et al. 📅 2026-05-27 PMID: 42200685
This retrospective study of 271 CKD patients (stages 3–5) developed and validated a combined cardiac/lung ultrasound diagnostic model to differentiate cardiogenic pulmonary edema (CPE) from non-cardiogenic dyspnea. Key independent predictors included E/e', IVC diameter, bilateral symmetric B-line pattern, consolidation, and pleural effusion; the model achieved AUC 0.97 in training and 0.89 in validation. For emergency physicians managing dyspneic dialysis-dependent patients, this integrated POCUS approach provides a validated tool to avoid inappropriate diuretic administration.
Abstract (verbatim from PubMed) Lung ultrasound (LUS) quantifying B-lines is valuable to assess fluid status during hemodialysis. However, B-lines may indicate not only cardiogenic pulmonary edema (CPE) but also non-cardiogenic pulmonary diseases, such as pneumonia or interstitial lung disease, posing challenges to guiding fluid therapy decisions. We developed a combined cardiac-LUS diagnostic model to differentiate these etiologies, aiming to prevent misdiagnosis and optimize treatment. This retrospective observational study enrolled chronic kidney disease (CKD) stage 3-5 patients divided into training and validation cohorts. Participants were classified into CPE group or non-cardiogenic dyspnea (NCD) group. Multivariable logistic regression identified discriminative echocardiographic and LUS parameters to build a diagnostic model. Predictive performance was assessed using receiver operator characteristic (ROC), calibration and decision curve analysis. This retrospective study analyzed 215 training and 56 validation participants. Multivariable regression identified E/e' (OR 1.180), inferior vena cava diameter (OR 1.240), bilateral symmetric B-pattern (OR 9.720), bilateral continuous B-pattern (OR 13.777), ≥1 irregular pleural line region (OR 0.078), ≥1 consolidation region (OR 0.039), ≥1 pleural effusion region (OR 4.732) as independent CPE predictors. The nomogram showed AUC of 0.9703 (training) and 0.8910 (validation). The nomogram model demonstrated reliable clinical application value and high predictive accuracy.

The Predictive Value of Different Lung Ultrasound Protocols for Extravascular Lung Water in Septic Shock Patients
Ding Y et al. 📅 2026-05-29 PMID: 42212423
In 46 septic shock patients with PiCCO monitoring, this study compared three LUS scoring protocols (4-region, 8-region, BLUE) against extravascular lung water index (EVLWI). The BLUE protocol demonstrated the strongest correlation with EVLWI (r=0.634 vs. 0.458 and 0.546 for 4-region and 8-region), with superior performance for predicting pulmonary edema at all severity thresholds. For POCUS users in septic shock resuscitation, this supports BLUE as the preferred LUS protocol for real-time lung water quantification.
Abstract (verbatim from PubMed) Currently, the role of lung ultrasound (LUS) in the diagnosis and treatment of patients with septic shock is widely recognized. Various LUS protocols and scoring criteria have been proposed, yet a unified LUS protocol for assessing lung water in these patients remains lacking. Forty-six septic shock patients underwent LUS with three scanning schemes (4-region, 8-region, BLUE) alongside pulse indicated continuous cardiac output (PiCCO) monitoring. Spearman correlation analysis was used to compare the correlation between the three ultrasound scoring schemes, PiCCO and other clinical laboratory indicators. Spearman's correlation analysis showed a significant correlation between extravascular lung water index (EVLWI) and 4-region, 8-region, or the Bedside Lung Ultrasound Examination (BLUE) protocol. More importantly, the BLUE protocol showed a stronger correlation with EVLWI than the 4-region protocol and the 8-region protocol (r=0.634, 0.458, 0.546, p<0.001). The ROC curve analysis showed that ultrasound protocols could predict the early occurrence of pulmonary edema in septic shock patients (EVLWI>7 ml/kg), diagnosis of pulmonary edema (EVLWI>10 ml/kg), and evaluation of pulmonary fluid severity (EVLWI≥15 ml/kg). Our study found that septic shock patients were prone to pulmonary edema during fluid resuscitation, and ultrasound scoring could help quantify pulmonary edema. Among the 4-region, 8-region and BLUE protocols, the BLUE protocol shows relatively better overall performance in predicting lung water elevation and evaluating edema severity.

Ultrasound-Guided Peripheral Nerve Blocks for Percutaneous Treatments of Common Tendinopathies
Ribeiro-da-Silva T et al. 📅 2026-01-02 PMID: 41479369
This practical review proposes anatomy-based ultrasound-guided peripheral nerve block (USG-PNB) approaches as procedural analgesia for common tendinopathies — including supraspinatus, epicondylitis, gluteal, patellar, Achilles, and plantar fasciopathy procedures. Unlike peritendinous local anesthetics, nerve blocks avoid potential tenocyte toxicity and can preserve orthobiologic efficacy. Emergency and sports medicine physicians performing bedside tendinopathy interventions will find this anatomic protocol guide directly useful.
Abstract (verbatim from PubMed) Ultrasound-guided percutaneous needle fenestration (UPNF) and percutaneous needle tenotomy (UPNT) are minimally invasive procedures commonly used to treat chronic tendinopathies. However, these techniques can be painful, potentially limiting patient tolerance, satisfaction, and procedural feasibility. Although local anesthetics (LAs) provide analgesia, their potential inhibitory effects on tenocyte activity and the efficacy of orthobiologics raise concerns regarding their impact on tendon healing. The objectives of this study is to propose a practical, anatomy-based approach for the use of ultrasound-guided peripheral nerve blocks (USG-PNBs) as a strategy to reduce pain and improve patient comfort during UPNF and UPNT, with or without orthobiologic adjuncts. We reviewed commonly affected anatomical sites in chronic tendinopathies and identified corresponding peripheral nerve targets that can be reliably and safely blocked under ultrasound guidance. For each condition, we outline appropriate nerve block techniques, anatomical landmarks, and recommended patient positioning to optimize procedural analgesia. A set of targeted USG-PNBs was defined for commonly treated tendinopathies, including supraspinatus tendinopathy, lateral and medial epicondylitis, gluteus medius and/or minimus tendinopathy, patellar tendinopathy, Achilles tendinopathy, and plantar fasciopathy. Ultrasound-guided peripheral nerve blocks represent a practical and effective approach to manage pain during percutaneous procedures for chronic tendinopathies. By reducing procedural discomfort and avoiding the potential drawbacks of peritendinous LAs, these techniques may enhance patient tolerance, procedural success, and overall clinical outcomes.

Automated Echocardiographic Detection of Congenital Heart Disease Using Artificial Intelligence (EchoFocus-CHD)
Lukyanenko P, Ghelani SJ et al. 📅 2026-03-28 PMID: 41902792
Trained on 3.4 million echo videos from 54,727 studies at Boston Children's Hospital, EchoFocus-CHD detects 12 critical and 8 noncritical congenital heart disease lesions with internal AUROC 0.94 for critical CHD. External validation revealed domain shift (AUROC 0.77), mitigated by retraining (AUROC 0.87). This landmark Circulation study demonstrates that AI can perform expert-level CHD echocardiographic interpretation and has profound implications for extending cardiac diagnostic capability globally — including to point-of-care settings.
Abstract (verbatim from PubMed) Delayed or missed diagnosis of congenital heart disease (CHD) contributes to excess pediatric mortality worldwide. Echocardiography (echo) is central to diagnosing and triaging CHD, yet expert interpretation remains a scarce and maldistributed global resource. Artificial intelligence offers the potential to democratize diagnostics and to extend expert-level interpretation beyond large academic centers, but its application in CHD remains underexplored. We developed EchoFocus-CHD, an artificial intelligence-enabled model for automated detection of 12 critical and 8 noncritical CHD lesions, individually and as composites. The composite critical CHD outcome was the primary end point. The model expands on a multitask, view-agnostic architecture (PanEcho) with a transformer encoder to improve focus on relevant echo views. The model was internally trained (80%) and tested (20%) on the first echo per patient from Boston Children's Hospital, with further evaluation on a referral cohort of echo studies performed at external US and international centers. The internal and referral cohorts included 3.4 million videos from 54,727 echos (median age at echo, 7.1 years; 5.8% critical CHD, 23.6% noncritical CHD) and 167,484 videos from 3356 echos (median age at echo, 2.5 years; 29.4% critical CHD, 45.6% noncritical CHD), respectively. EchoFocus-CHD showed excellent internal ability to detect the composite critical CHD outcome (AUROC, 0.94; positive likelihood ratio, 7.50; negative likelihood ratio, 0.14) and individual critical lesions (AUROC, 0.83-1.00), as well as composite noncritical CHD (AUROC, 0.90). Performance declined during evaluation on the referral cohort to detect critical CHD (AUROC, 0.77), coinciding with greater expert disagreement on referral cases. Retraining on all available US patients attenuated domain shift effects, improving international critical CHD detection (AUROC, 0.87). EchoFocus-CHD shows promise for automated CHD detection to advance equitable global cardiovascular care and highlights the need to address domain shift and to establish external validation before real-world deployment.

Smarter FoCUS: AI-Guided Focused Cardiac Ultrasound Enables Novice Detection of Left Ventricular Dysfunction
Kane CJ et al. (Mayo Clinic) 📅 2026-05-13 PMID: 42169733
In this prospective Mayo Clinic study (496 patients + 344 in validation), novice operators with just 4 hours of training performed AI-guided handheld FoCUS (Philips Lumify + UltraSight real-time guidance + AI interpretation) to assess LVEF. A two-step process (AI reads, experts review only abnormal/uninterpretable) achieved sensitivity 96.2%, specificity 95.4%, NPV 99.8%; prospective validation confirmed sensitivity 100%, specificity 99.4%. This demonstrates that AI-guided POCUS with minimal training can reliably screen for reduced LV function at population scale — a potential game-changer for cardiac screening programs.
Abstract (verbatim from PubMed) Heart failure is prevalent; however, there is no cost-effective screening option. Against formal echocardiography, we assessed the diagnostic performance of focused cardiac ultrasound (FoCUS), performed by novice users guided and interpreted by artificial intelligence (AI) for the assessment of left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF). We prospectively enrolled 496 adults referred for diagnostic echocardiography. Novice operators (without clinical or imaging experience) underwent a 4-h imaging workshop, then performed FoCUS using a point-of-care device (Philips Lumify) utilizing real-time AI-image guidance (UltraSight). Images were assessed by AI-image interpretation software (Mayo Clinic), and two expert blinded echocardiologists. Forty-one subjects (8.3%) exhibited moderate or greater LV dilatation and 28 (5.6%) had LVEF <40%. The median scan time was 4 min. Adequate views were achieved in 95.0% and 97.4% of subjects for AI interpretation and expert analysis, respectively. A two-step diagnostic screening process for low EF in which only abnormal AI reads or uninterpretable scans were reviewed by experts (15.1%) had a sensitivity 96.2%, specificity 95.4%, PPV 54.3%, and NPV 99.8%. A subsequent prospective validation study of 344 subjects demonstrated similar results (sensitivity 100%, specificity 99.4%). In this early feasibility investigation, AI-guidance technology embedded on a handheld device enabled novice users without clinical or imaging experience to acquire sufficient quality images to accurately assess LVEF with minimal training. The technology evaluated in this study may hold promise for AI-guidance and interpretation to facilitate low-cost screening for cardiac dysfunction.

Abstract (verbatim from PubMed) We aimed to develop and evaluate fully automated artificial intelligence (AI) system for detection of mitral valve prolapse (MVP) and mitral regurgitation (MR) from echocardiographic studies. These algorithms could provide a novel approach for automated, accurate, and rapid diagnosis of MVP and its common clinical sequelae across institutions.

Abstract (verbatim from PubMed) Ultrasound image segmentation remains a significant challenge due to inherent low contrast and blurred anatomical boundaries. This study aims to develop an effective semi-supervised segmentation framework for ultrasound images with limited annotations. We propose a novel semi-supervised segmentation framework tailored for ultrasound images, leveraging frequency component augmentation and edge mask enhancement to promote structural consistency between weakly and strongly augmented inputs.
